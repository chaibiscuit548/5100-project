Introduction

Understanding how economic perceptions shape mental and physical well-being is a central concern in the social sciences. Periods of economic uncertainty often coincide with higher levels of psychological distress, worsened health outcomes, and declining institutional trust. This analysis examines the relationship between Americans feelings of financial security and their self-reported mental health using data from the General Social Survey (GSS). The GSS provides one of the most comprehensive long-term datasets available for studying the attitudes, behaviors, and health outcomes of U.S. adults, making it uniquely well suited for evaluating how financial attitudes correspond to health-related quality of life.

Our investigation focuses on two dependent variables collected by the GSS:

  1. mntlhlth the number of days in the past 30 during which respondents experienced poor mental health.

  2. hlthdays the number of days during which poor health limited usual activities.

These are compared against three financial-attitude measures:

  • confinan confidence in financial institutions.

  • satfin satisfaction with ones financial situation.

  • finalter whether the respondent feels financially better, worse, or the same compared to the previous year.

Because GSS health questions were not asked every year, the analysis focuses on years in which the mental-health and activity-limitation modules were administered. Mental-health data (mntlhlth) are available for 2002, 2004, 2006, 2010, 2012, 2014, 2016, 2018, and 2022, while activity-limitation data (hlthdays) appear for 2002, 2006, 2010, 2014, 2018, and 2022. Restricting the dataset to these years preserves comparability and avoids introducing bias from missing survey waves.

Negative values in GSS health variables represent nonvalid codes (such as Inapplicable or Not asked) rather than true values. These were recoded as NA to prevent them from being treated as genuine observations. The code below shows the initial data preparation, including numeric conversion, removal of invalid responses, and extraction of the relevant survey years.

GSS_clean <- GSS %>%
  
  # Race: keep only White, Black, Other
  mutate(race = ifelse(race %in% c("White", "Black", "Other"), race, NA)) %>%
  
  # Income: turn missing codes into NA
  mutate(income = ifelse(income %in% c(".i:  Inapplicable",
                                       ".d:  Do not Know/Cannot Choose",
                                       ".r:  Refused",
                                       ".n:  No answer",
                                       ".s:  Skipped on Web"),
                         NA, income)) %>%
  
  # Happy: keep only 3 valid levels, others to NA
  mutate(happy = ifelse(happy %in% c("Not too happy", "Pretty happy", "Very happy"),
                        happy, NA)) %>%
  
  # Confidence variables (each keeps only 3 valid responses)
  mutate(confinan  = ifelse(confinan  %in% c("A GREAT DEAL", "ONLY SOME", "HARDLY ANY"), confinan,  NA),
         conbus    = ifelse(conbus    %in% c("A GREAT DEAL", "ONLY SOME", "HARDLY ANY"), conbus,    NA),
         confed    = ifelse(confed    %in% c("A GREAT DEAL", "ONLY SOME", "HARDLY ANY"), confed,    NA),
         conlegis  = ifelse(conlegis  %in% c("A GREAT DEAL", "ONLY SOME", "HARDLY ANY"), conlegis,  NA)) %>%
  
  # Satisfaction with finances
  mutate(satfin = ifelse(satfin %in% c("Not satisfied at all",
                                       "More or less satisfied",
                                       "Pretty well satisfied"),
                         satfin, NA)) %>%
  
  # Financial alternatives
  mutate(finalter = ifelse(finalter %in% c("Better", "Stayed same", "Worse"),
                           finalter, NA)) %>%
  
  # Relative financial situation
  mutate(finrela = ifelse(finrela %in% c("AVERAGE", "ABOVE AVERAGE", "BELOW AVERAGE",
                                         "FAR ABOVE AVERAGE", "FAR BELOW AVERAGE"),
                          finrela, NA)) %>%
  
  # Mental health: turn missing codes into NA
  mutate(mntlhlth = ifelse(mntlhlth %in% c(".i:  Inapplicable",
                                           ".d:  Do not Know/Cannot Choose",
                                           ".n:  No answer"),

                                           ".d:  Do not Know/Cannot Choose",

Before conducting statistical tests, normality and variance assumptions were evaluated. ShapiroWilk tests were applied within each financialattitude category. The results showed extremely small p-values (all < 0.001), indicating that the distribution of mental-health days is highly non-normal across all groups. Levenes tests similarly revealed large and significant variance differences across categories of financial trust, satisfaction, and financial change. As a consequence, parametric tests such as ANOVA and t-tests would not be appropriate, and non-parametric approaches were used instead.

Results

Visualization of Mental Health and Financial Attitudes

To begin evaluating how financial trust and financial well-being relate to mental health, we first examined distributional differences in mental health outcomes across categories of financial confidence, financial satisfaction, and perceived financial trajectory. We used two measures of mental health that the GSS collects: the number of poor mental health days (mntlhlth) and the number of activity-limitation days (hlthdays). Because both variables represent counts of days within the past month, many respondents reported zero days. We therefore present results twice: once with all non-missing values and once with zero values excluded, since zero often reflects an absence of symptoms rather than variation in severity.

1. Poor Mental Health Days by Financial Attitudes (All Values Included)

Finding: The first figure shows that respondents with lower trust in financial institutions, poorer financial satisfaction, and worse year-to-year financial change tend to experience more poor mental health days.

Across the trust categories (A Great Deal, Only Some, Hardly Any), individuals with Hardly Any trust report noticeably higher median poor-mental-health days and a broader spread of values. The same pattern appears in financial satisfaction: those Not Satisfied at All show substantially higher mental health burden than individuals who are Pretty Well Satisfied. For financial change, respondents who say their situation became Worse report the highest levels of poor mental health.

Overall, the figure suggests a clear gradient: as financial well-being indicators worsen, the distribution of poor mental health days shifts upward.

2. Activity Limitation Days by Financial Attitudes (All Values Included)

Finding: A parallel pattern appears for limited-activity days. Although fewer respondents report high values, the central tendency follows the same direction. The Hardly Any trust group, the Not Satisfied at All group, and the Worse group consistently show more limited-activity days than their financially better-off counterparts.

These results indicate that financial strain and lack of confidence correspond not only to emotional distress but also to measurable physical or functional limitations.

3. Poor Mental Health Days by Financial Attitudes (Zero Excluded)

Finding: After removing the large cluster of respondents who reported zero poor mental health days, the same gradients persist but become even clearer. Among individuals experiencing at least one mentally unhealthy day, the Hardly Any trust group displays a distinct upward shift. The Worse financial-change group shows the most pronounced elevation in both median values and upper tails. Respondents who are Not Satisfied at All with their finances also report markedly higher symptom burdens than those who are financially satisfied.

The exclusion of zeros reveals a sharper contrast in distributions, strengthening the argument that financial insecurity is linked to elevated mental health burdens among those who experience symptoms.

4. Activity Limitation Days by Financial Attitudes (Zero Excluded)

Finding: The final figure provides the same analysis for activity-limitation days, again excluding zero values. The patterns remain consistent. Low confidence in financial institutions and worsening financial conditions are associated with more days of physical or functional difficulty. In particular, individuals reporting that their financial situation became Worse show the highest median and the widest range of activity-limitation days.

This consistency across two different health measures indicates that the relationship between financial security and health is not limited to a single domain. Financial stress appears to affect both emotional well-being and the ability to perform usual daily activities.

Statistical Testing of Mental Health and Financial Attitudes

1. Normality and Variance Diagnostics

Before conducting group comparisons, we tested whether mental health outcomes followed a normal distribution within each financial category. The Shapiro tests for poor mental health days showed extremely small p-values for all levels of financial satisfaction, confidence in financial institutions, and perceptions of financial change (all p < 1e-27). These values indicate that the distributions are strongly non-normal across every grouping. The same pattern held for activity-limitation days.

We also tested the assumption of equal variances using Levenes test. Variances were significantly different across categories of financial satisfaction, financial confidence, and financial trajectory for the poor mental health variable (all p < 1e-9). Variances differed for two out of three predictors for activity-limitation days as well. The combination of non-normality and unequal variances shows that parametric methods such as t-tests or ANOVA are not appropriate for these data.

Given these violations, we proceeded with non-parametric analyses.

2. KruskalWallis Tests

Poor Mental Health Days (mntlhlth)

For respondents reporting at least one poor mental health day, all three financial variables showed significant differences across categories:

  • Confidence in financial institutions: H(2) = 19.50, p = 5.8e-05

  • Financial satisfaction: H(2) = 197.44, p < 2.2e-16

  • Perceived financial change: H(2) = 84.32, p < 2.2e-16

These results indicate that poor mental health days differ systematically depending on financial confidence, satisfaction, and whether finances improved, stayed the same, or became worse.

Activity-Limitation Days (hlthdays)

For activity-limitation days, two of the three variables showed significant differences:

  • Confidence in financial institutions: H(2) = 1.78, p = 0.41 (not significant)

  • Financial satisfaction: H(2) = 64.00, p = 1.27e-14

  • Perceived financial change: H(2) = 47.42, p = 5.04e-11

These results show that activity-limitation days vary by financial satisfaction and by perceived financial change, but not by trust in financial institutions.

3. Dunn Post-Hoc Tests

Mental Health Days

Financial confidence: Significant pairwise differences emerged between

  • A Great Deal vs Hardly Any (p = 0.0016)

  • Hardly Any vs Only Some (p = 0.00015)

The contrast between A Great Deal and Only Some was not significant. This pattern suggests that the largest differences occur between the most skeptical group and the others.

Financial satisfaction: All three pairwise comparisons were significant after adjustment:

  • More or less satisfied vs Not satisfied at all (p 1.4e-28)

  • More or less satisfied vs Pretty well satisfied (p 4.4e-04)

  • Not satisfied at all vs Pretty well satisfied (p 4.1e-37)

This is a very strong gradient, with dissatisfaction associated with sharply worse mental health outcomes.

Perceived financial change: All comparisons were significant:

  • Better vs Stayed the same (p 0.0499)

  • Better vs Worse (p 3.4e-19)

  • Stayed the same vs Worse (p 2.6e-10)

People whose financial situation worsened report much higher mental health burdens.

Activity-Limitation Days

Financial confidence: None of the pairwise contrasts were significant (all adjusted p > 0.72). This is consistent with the non-significant Kruskal result.

Financial satisfaction: Significant differences were found between

  • More or less satisfied vs Not satisfied at all (p 1.6e-11)

  • Not satisfied at all vs Pretty well satisfied (p 3.8e-11)

There was no significant difference between More or less satisfied and Pretty well satisfied.

Perceived financial change: Two pairwise comparisons were significant:

  • Better vs Worse (p 2.0e-11)

  • Stayed the same vs Worse (p 2.8e-05)

The comparison between Better and Stayed the same was not significant after correction. People whose finances worsened have the highest activity-limitation burdens.

4. Spearman Correlations

Correlation with Poor Mental Health Days

Using the recoded ordinal versions of the financial variables (higher values represent more security or improvement), we find:

  • Financial confidence: rho = 0.081, p = 0.00011

  • Financial satisfaction: rho = 0.120, p = 1.9e-12

  • Perceived financial change: rho = 0.104, p = 8.3e-10

Although the correlations are modest in size, all three are statistically significant and negative. This means that worse financial conditions correspond to more poor mental health days.

Correlation with Activity-Limitation Days

  • Financial confidence: rho = 0.036, p = 0.19 (not significant)

  • Financial satisfaction: rho = 0.171, p = 2.46e-14

  • Perceived financial change: rho = 0.150, p = 2.43e-11

Again, financial satisfaction and perceived financial change show clear associations with physical health limitations, while financial confidence does not show a statistically meaningful relationship.

Summary of Statistical Findings

Across every analysis, one pattern appears repeatedly. People who feel financially insecure, financially dissatisfied, or financially worse off report higher levels of mental health distress and more days of physical limitation. The strongest associations involve financial satisfaction and financial trajectory, which consistently show large differences between groups and highly significant results in all non-parametric tests and correlations.

Trust in financial institutions shows weaker patterns. It is meaningfully related to poor mental health days but not to days of physical limitation. This suggests that the subjective experience of ones own financial situation plays a larger role in mental and physical health than trust in the broader financial system.
